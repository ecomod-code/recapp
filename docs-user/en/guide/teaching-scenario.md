# Recapp in Practice: Recapitulation Across Two Sessions <Badge type="tip" text="Teachers" />

This page describes the teaching scenario Recapp was originally designed for: using student-generated questions as a structured recapitulation exercise across two course sessions. If you're new to Recapp, reading this before the how-to guides will help everything else make more sense.

## The idea

Students remember material better when they engage with it actively and from multiple angles. This scenario creates two recapitulation moments built into the natural rhythm of your course:

- **End of session 1** — Students write quiz questions about what they just learned. Formulating a good question requires understanding the material, not just recognising it.
- **Start of session 2** — Everyone answers each other's questions. Students encounter the material again, now from their peers' perspectives rather than yours.

The result is that the same content gets revisited twice, in two different ways, with minimal extra effort once the quiz is set up.

## What you need

- Internet-enabled devices for all participants (smartphones work fine)
- A course of roughly 5–50 students. Larger groups are technically possible, but keep the question set manageable — if you have 80 students each submitting two questions, curating and answering 160 questions in one session isn't realistic. For larger courses, have students work in groups and submit one question per group.

## Setting up the quiz

Create a blank quiz in Recapp before your session. The only setting you **must** enable is **Allow participants to set questions** — without it, students can't submit anything. Everything else is optional, but these settings are worth thinking about:

**Allowed question types** — Single- and multiple-choice questions work best for recapitulation because they force students to think about wrong answers too, not just correct ones. Consider disabling free-text questions for this scenario.

**Allow participant comments** — Useful if you want students to flag confusion or ask follow-up questions during the answering phase. You can address these comments during the class discussion.

**Participants can view statistics** — If you leave this off, students only see their own results after finishing, not the group results. This gives you more control over the debrief — everyone looks at the class results together on the projector rather than individually on their phones.

**Hide comment list** — If comments are enabled but you want students to focus on answering without reading others' comments first, hide the comment list during the answering phase and reveal it during the discussion.

## Running the scenario

### End of session 1: collect questions

Share the quiz link or QR code with students and give them a clear time frame — for example, "until the end of today's session" or "by midnight tonight."

A few things worth communicating to students:
- Each person should submit at least one question (set your own expectation — one is the minimum, two or three is better)
- For choice questions, they need to mark the correct answer(s) — show a quick example if this is their first time
- Their name on the question is only visible to you, not to other students
- Why this matters: writing questions reveals what you actually understand versus what you think you understand, and it gives the whole group a way to review the material from multiple angles

::: tip Set a deadline and stick to it
Students need to know when question submission closes, and you need time to curate before the next session. A same-day or overnight deadline works well.
:::

### Between sessions: curate

Once the submission window closes, switch the quiz to curation mode. Go through the submitted questions and:
- Fix unclear wording or incorrect answer options
- Remove duplicates or questions that are too vague to be useful
- Reorder questions if a particular sequence makes sense
- Add your own questions if there are gaps

This is the most time-intensive part of running the scenario, especially the first time. It gets faster once you know what kinds of issues to expect.

### Start of session 2: run the quiz

Start the quiz at the beginning of the session. Students answer at their own pace — set a time limit and announce it clearly (try it out beforehand to find a realistic duration for your question set).

While students work, you can monitor the statistical evaluation tab in real time. You'll see which questions are causing the most difficulty before the debrief even begins.

### Debrief: discuss the results

Stop the quiz and switch to presentation mode to project the results. Walk through the questions — focus on the ones with the most wrong answers, or the ones that generated the most comments.

Some ways to use the statistics in the discussion:
- *"I can see a lot of people found question 3 difficult — let's look at that one first."*
- *"Many of your questions touched on topic X, which is actually where today's session begins."*
- *"There are several comments here about Y — let's address those."*

If comments were enabled, prioritise the most upvoted ones. These represent the questions the group collectively found most pressing.

## Variants

### Most important insight

Instead of asking students to write a question about anything from the session, ask them to identify their single most important insight and write a question about that. This adds an extra reflection step — students have to evaluate what mattered most before they can write the question.

Be aware: this works less well if the session's most important takeaways were procedural or methodological rather than factual. Students struggle to write quiz questions about processes.

### Active listening during the lecture

Open the (empty) quiz at the start of a lecture rather than the end. Students join via link or QR code and add questions during the lecture itself as ideas occur to them. This encourages active engagement with the material as it's being taught.

Build in short pauses — a minute or two — so students can formulate questions without losing track of the lecture. These breaks are often useful in themselves.

The questions can be answered directly after the lecture, collectively at the next session, or simply collected without being answered if the goal is question-writing as a learning technique rather than a full quiz.

## Limitations

- **Requires devices and wifi** — if your room has poor connectivity or students don't have smartphones, the logistics get complicated
- **Takes time in two sessions** — the question-writing phase and the debrief both need protected time; this doesn't work as an afterthought at the end of an overrunning session
- **Curation takes effort** — especially with larger groups or when students are new to the format and submit vague or poorly structured questions
- **Student motivation varies** — writing questions at the end of a long session is a creative effort and some students will resist it, especially the first time. It tends to work better once students have done it once and seen how the results are used
- **Non-participation is hard to spot** — unlike a spoken activity, it's not obvious in the moment who hasn't submitted anything
